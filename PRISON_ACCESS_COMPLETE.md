# ABSTRACT

A secure memorial access system enables incarcerated individuals to view digital memorials of deceased loved ones through monitored kiosk terminals in correctional facilities. The system comprises a memorial platform hosting photos, videos, and tributes; a prison access module integrating with existing kiosk infrastructure; identity verification using prison credentials; payment processing through commissary accounts and third-party prison payment vendors (JPay, GTL, ViaPath); time-limited session management; comprehensive activity logging; content filtering for security; and administrative oversight dashboards. Incarcerated individuals request access to specific memorials, pay on a per-minute basis ($0.15-$0.30/minute), and view memorial content in secure sessions (15-60 minutes). Families can pre-approve access or respond to access requests. All activity is monitored and logged for security compliance. Revenue is automatically shared between the platform operator and correctional facilities (typically 70/30 split). The system supports multiple facilities simultaneously with facility-specific configurations, adapts to different payment vendors, and scales across federal, state, and local correctional institutions. This invention solves the problem of digital isolation during grief, maintains family connections critical for rehabilitation, generates revenue for facilities, and operates in a zero-competition market serving 2.3 million incarcerated individuals.

# FIELD OF THE INVENTION

This invention relates to secure digital memorial access systems, specifically enabling incarcerated individuals to access memorial pages of deceased loved ones via monitored kiosks in correctional facilities while maintaining institutional security requirements and generating revenue through time-based access fees.

# BACKGROUND OF THE INVENTION

## The Problem: Digital Exclusion in Corrections

The United States incarcerates approximately 2.3 million individuals across federal prisons, state prisons, local jails, and other correctional facilities. These individuals face severe restrictions on internet access due to legitimate security concerns, including prevention of criminal coordination, gang communication, victim harassment, and unauthorized information disclosure.

However, this digital isolation creates a devastating unintended consequence: when a family member passes away, incarcerated individuals cannot access online memorial platforms, view photos and videos of their deceased loved ones, read tributes from other family members, or participate in the digital grieving process that has become standard in modern society.

## Current State of Prison Technology

Correctional facilities currently provide limited technology services through three primary vendors:

1. **JPay (owned by Securus Technologies)** - Provides email, video visitation, and money transfer services
2. **GTL (Global Tel Link)** - Offers phone services, messaging, and commissary management
3. **ViaPath Technologies** - Delivers communication and education services

These systems operate on a pay-per-use model, typically charging $0.15-$0.30 per minute for services. All communications are monitored, logged, and subject to security review. However, none of these vendors provide memorial access services.

## Existing Memorial Platforms Are Inaccessible

Several digital memorial platforms exist in the consumer market:

1. **Legacy.com** - Aggregates obituaries from funeral homes and newspapers, offering online memorial pages with photo galleries and tribute walls
2. **Ever Loved** - Provides memorial fundraising and photo sharing
3. **Memories.net** - Hosts virtual memorials with limited features
4. **ForeverMissed.com** - Free memorial websites with basic functionality

While these platforms serve families well, they share critical limitations for the incarcerated population:

- **Web-based access only** - Require unrestricted internet browsing, which is prohibited in correctional facilities
- **No prison integration** - Cannot interface with existing prison kiosk systems
- **No security monitoring** - Lack activity logging and oversight capabilities required by correctional administrators
- **No payment integration** - Cannot process payments through prison commissary accounts
- **No identity verification** - Cannot authenticate users via prison ID systems

## The Emotional and Social Impact

When an incarcerated individual loses a family member, the inability to access memorial content causes:

1. **Prolonged grief** - Unable to view photos that help with closure
2. **Family disconnection** - Cannot see tributes from relatives they may have lost contact with
3. **Psychological distress** - Feelings of abandonment and isolation intensify
4. **Rehabilitation barriers** - Family connection is crucial for successful reentry; severing these bonds during grief undermines rehabilitation efforts

Research from the Prison Policy Initiative and The Marshall Project has documented that maintaining family connections during incarceration significantly reduces recidivism rates. The Last Mile, a nonprofit organization founded in 2010, has demonstrated that technology access for positive purposes (education, family connection) improves outcomes without compromising security.

## The Market Opportunity

The intersection of corrections technology and memorial services represents an untapped market:

- **2.3 million potential users** in U.S. correctional facilities
- **Annual mortality context** - Each incarcerated individual likely experiences the death of 1-2 family members during average incarceration period (2-5 years for state inmates, 12.4 years for federal inmates)
- **Willingness to pay** - Incarcerated individuals already spend $1.6 billion annually on communication services (phone, email, video visits), demonstrating proven payment behavior
- **Facility revenue sharing** - Correctional facilities receive 30-50% commission on all services, creating financial incentive for adoption
- **Zero competition** - No existing vendor offers memorial access services

At a conservative estimate of $3.00 per session (20 minutes at $0.15/minute), with 2.3 million individuals accessing memorials an average of 2 times per year, the total addressable market exceeds $13.8 million annually.

## Technical Challenges Requiring Innovation

Creating a memorial access system for correctional facilities requires solving several novel technical challenges:

**Challenge 1: Security vs. Access Balance**
The system must provide meaningful memorial access while preventing security risks such as:
- Smuggling contraband information through memorial photos
- Gang communication via memorial tribute walls
- Coordination of criminal activity through memorial pages
- Harassment of crime victims who may appear in memorial content
- Screenshot capture and unauthorized content distribution

**Challenge 2: Identity Verification Without Privacy Violation**
The system must authenticate that a user has a legitimate relationship to the deceased without:
- Exposing sensitive family information
- Requiring complex verification that delays access
- Creating administrative burden for correctional staff
- Violating HIPAA or privacy regulations

**Challenge 3: Payment Processing Across Multiple Vendors**
The system must integrate with multiple prison payment providers:
- JPay accounts (Securus Technologies)
- GTL commissary systems
- ViaPath payment systems
- State-specific proprietary systems (e.g., California CDCR accounts)
- Federal Bureau of Prisons TRULINCS system

Each vendor uses different APIs, payment flows, and technical specifications.

**Challenge 4: Content Moderation at Scale**
Memorial pages may contain:
- Thousands of photos uploaded by multiple family members
- Videos with audio that could contain coded messages
- Tribute text that could include inappropriate content
- Links to external websites

The system must filter and moderate this content without creating excessive delays or false positives that frustrate legitimate users.

**Challenge 5: Cross-Jurisdictional Deployment**
The United States has:
- 122 federal prisons (Bureau of Prisons)
- 1,566 state prisons across 50 states
- 2,850+ local jails
- 1,510 juvenile correctional facilities
- 80 Indian Country jails
- Military prisons

Each jurisdiction has different:
- Technology infrastructure (kiosk vendors, network capabilities)
- Security protocols and approval processes
- Payment systems and commission structures
- Legal requirements and privacy regulations

A viable solution must be adaptable to this fragmented landscape without requiring custom development for each facility.
